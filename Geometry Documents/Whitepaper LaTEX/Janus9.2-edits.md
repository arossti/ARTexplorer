## New Ideas for v10 (from our Feb 13 discussion)

### Idea 1: The Paired-Space Thesis (Reclaimed from v8, sharpened)

When a tetrahedron contracts through the origin, the sign flip produces re-expansion into a **structurally identical but orientation-reversed space**. This is not one space with a sign convention — it is a **paired structure**: two complete geometric arenas connected at the origin.

What makes the tetrahedral case special (vs. generic overcomplete bases): the tet is the minimum closed form, so its inversion produces the **dual** (vertices ↔ face-centers), not a rotated copy. The cube inverted is still a cube (centrosymmetric). The tetrahedron inverted is *something else*.

**v10 framing:** State this directly. Don't apologize for it. Acknowledge it's a geometric hypothesis, not a proven theorem — but present it as the paper's central proposal, not as one of three hedged tiers.

### Idea 2: The Inversion Manifold (New in v10)

The origin is not unique. **Any point can serve as a local inversion locus** through which a form contracts, inverts, and re-expands. This reframes Janus Inversion from a single coordinate trick to a **structural principle**: space is perforated with potential inversion points at every scale.

- **Macro scale:** A black hole is a region where matter contracts through a local origin — the Janus question is "what's on the other side?"
- **Micro scale:** A photon (zero rest mass, zero spatial extent in its own frame) **is** a Janus point — it exists at the boundary between the two states
- The "manifold of possible inversions" isn't a single operation but a **field** of potential origin-points, each connecting a positive and negative arena

This was implicit in v8's "Scale-Invariant Janus Points" section but never developed. v10 should make it a first-class concept.

### Idea 3: The Three-State Geometry (New in v10)

Janus Inversion produces a natural three-state structure:

1. **+** (positive parity / expansion / the outward tetrahedron)
2. **−** (negative parity / inverted expansion / the dual tetrahedron)
3. **Origin** (the singular boundary / zero extent / neither-and-both)

This is structurally parallel to quantum spin:

1. **Spin up** (+)
2. **Spin down** (−)
3. **Superposition** (pre-measurement: contains both possibilities)

The structural parallels:
- The observer **must be on one side** — you can't observe from the origin because the origin has zero spatial extent. This mirrors the quantum measurement problem: you can't observe superposition directly.
- The two states are **functionally identical but distinguishable** — just as spin-up and spin-down electrons are identical except for orientation relative to the measurement axis.
- The symmetry between the states is **exact** — neither + nor − is privileged.
- **Observation and polarity are inextricably related** — the act of choosing a vantage point (which side of the origin) determines which state you see.

Where to be careful (avoiding Woo):
- Quantum superposition is a probability amplitude in Hilbert space, not a geometric coexistence. The analogy is **structural** (three-state logic) rather than **mechanical** (the physics of decoherence).
- "Observation" means different things: physical interaction (QM) vs. choosing a vantage point (Janus). Related conceptually, not equivalent.
- We are noting an **isomorphism in the logic**, not claiming that Janus Inversion *is* quantum mechanics.

**v10 framing:** Present the three-state structure as a geometric primitive. Note the quantum parallel honestly. Don't claim causation or identity — claim structural resonance. Let the reader draw implications.

## Terminology Discipline (agreed Feb 13)

Consistent usage throughout the paper — don't mix levels within a paragraph:

1. **Basis vectors** — Pure directions. No numbers, no magnitudes, no metric. They have angular relationships (spreads) between them. The Quadray basis vectors are the four tetrahedral rays; the Cartesian basis vectors are i, j, k.

2. **Coordinate space** — Created when we assign a *scale* (unit of measurement) to basis vectors. This is a deliberate step that introduces numbers. The choice of scale determines which relationships are rational (see Rationality Reciprocity).

3. **Coordinates** — The numerical address of a specific point within a coordinate space. A tuple of values. Use `(x,y,z)` for Cartesian, `[w,x,y,z]` for Quadray — visually distinct notation throughout.

Terms to use precisely:
- "basis vector" — direction only
- "coordinate" — numerical value for a point
- "spread" — rational angular measure (not "angle" unless referencing trig convention)
- "quadrance" — rational distance measure (not "distance" unless referencing metric convention)
- Don't say "component" when you mean "coordinate" or "value"
- Don't say "axis" when you mean "basis vector direction"

### 1. Cartan Geometry: A Complete Model Spacetime at Every Point

**Key references:**
- Elie Cartan (1920s-30s): method of moving frames
- Richard W. Sharpe, *Differential Geometry: Cartan's Generalization of Klein's Erlangen Program* (Springer GTM 166, 1997)
- Derek K. Wise, "MacDowell-Mansouri Gravity and Cartan Geometry" (Class. Quant. Grav. 27, 2010; arXiv: gr-qc/0611154)

**The idea:** In standard Riemannian geometry, each point p of a manifold M has a tangent space T_pM — a flat vector space. Cartan replaced this with a **full Klein geometry** G/H at every point. The Cartan connection provides the rule for "rolling" this model geometry along the manifold without slipping. Curvature measures the failure of this rolling to close around infinitesimal loops.

The slogan: *"Cartan geometry is to Klein geometry as Riemannian geometry is to Euclidean geometry."*

**Connection to Section 5.1:** This is Kieran's observation made rigorous. Cartan geometry enriches the tangent structure from a vector space to a **complete geometry with its own symmetry group**. If the model geometry's symmetry group G includes inversions (as it does when G contains O(n) rather than just SO(n)), then the tangent model at each point **already contains the inversion symmetry**. The Cartan connection transports this — including inversions — from point to point.

### 2. Pin Structures: Including Parity in the Double Cover

**Key references:**
- Atiyah, Bott & Shapiro (1964): Clifford modules and spinor classification
- Dabrowski & Trautman, "Spinor structures on spheres and projective spaces" (arXiv: 1709.02742)
- Freed & Hopkins (2016, 2021): reflection-positive invertible field theories

**The idea:** The rotation group SO(n) has a double cover called **Spin(n)** — what lets you define spinor fields (fermions). But SO(n) only handles proper rotations. The full orthogonal group O(n) includes reflections and parity reversal. Its double cover is the **Pin group**, with two inequivalent versions:

- **Pin+(n):** A reflection squares to +1
- **Pin-(n):** A reflection squares to -1

A Spin structure assigns a rotation double-cover fiber to each point. A **Pin structure** extends this to include **reflections and parity inversions** at every point.

**Connection to Section 5.1:** Pin structures are exactly the formalization of "including parity/inversion in the geometric structure at each point." The Z_2 parity of Janus Inversion (the tet-dual sign flip) maps onto the Pin group's extension of Spin. Whether a theory admits Pin+ or Pin- determines how fermions transform under parity (P) and time reversal (T) — the same discrete symmetries in the Boyle-Turok CPT cosmology already cited in Section 7.

This is the most algebraically direct formalization of the Janus parity label (the +/- binary state in 4D+/-).

## The Relational Bridge: Coordinates vs. Distances (Feb 15, 2026)

### The Distinction We Need to Address

Private correspondence has surfaced a sharp and productive distinction between two kinds of "all-positive" geometry:

1. **Representational positivity** — Quadray coordinates are all-positive because the tetrahedral basis vectors span 3D without requiring negative projections. The positivity is a property of the *coordinate system*.

2. **Relational positivity** — The mutual distances (or quadrances) between any finite collection of points are inherently positive. The positivity is a property of the *physical observables themselves*, independent of any coordinate choice.

The relational approach (associated with Leibniz's philosophy of space and formalized in modern Shape Dynamics) starts from the second kind: take all pairwise separations between N points, note that they are always positive, and observe that the correlations between them — the constraints they must satisfy to be embeddable in Euclidean space — *are* the geometry. No coordinates needed. The geometry is the correlation structure of positive relational quantities.

### Why This Matters for Our Paper

The current paper works exclusively with the first kind of positivity (representational). The Inversion Manifold, the three-state geometry, the Cartesian Blind Spot — all are framed in terms of coordinate signs. A relational physicist will read this and think: "Interesting coordinate trick, but I work without coordinates. What does your framework say in my language?"

This is not an objection to deflect. It is a question to answer.

### The Bridge: Rational Trigonometry as Relational Algebra

Here's the key observation: **Rational Trigonometry already IS the algebra of positive relational quantities.**

- **Quadrance** Q = d^2 is always positive — it is a relational observable (squared distance between two points), not a coordinate
- **Spread** s = sin^2(theta) is always positive — it is a relational observable (angular relationship between two lines), not a coordinate
- The **Triple Quad Formula**, **Spread Law**, and **Cross Law** operate entirely on these positive relational quantities using only +, -, *, /
- No coordinates appear in the laws themselves — they are statements about relationships between points and lines

So Wildberger's framework is already relational in exactly the sense that a Shape Dynamics practitioner would recognize. The question is: what does the tetrahedral basis contribute to this relational picture that a coordinate-free approach wouldn't see?

### The Specific Question for v10.4

**Can the Cayley-Menger constraints on N-body distances be expressed purely in RT terms?**

The Cayley-Menger determinant gives the necessary and sufficient conditions for a set of pairwise distances to be embeddable in Euclidean space of a given dimension. In standard form, it uses squared distances (i.e., quadrances). The conditions are polynomial — no square roots, no transcendentals.

This means:
- The embeddability constraints are already rational in RT's sense
- The "highly correlated positive separations" of N-body shape space are quadrance relations
- The tetrahedron (4 points, 6 pairwise quadrances, all expressible as rational functions of the tetrahedral spread 8/9) is the *minimal* such configuration in 3D

**The conjecture to develop:** The tetrahedral basis is not just a convenient coordinate system — it is the minimal structural system whose internal quadrance and spread relationships generate the Cayley-Menger constraints for 3D Euclidean embedding. The all-positive Quadray spanning is the coordinate expression of a deeper relational fact: four points in tetrahedral configuration are the minimum required to distinguish interior from exterior, and their pairwise quadrances are the minimum relational data needed to define 3D geometry.
